    Section A.1 Integration Rules

    Because of the fundamental theorem of calculus, every differentiation rule yields a corresponding integration rule. For example, the product rule for derivatives, yields the following:

    Theorem A.1.1. Integration by Parts.

    If \(u, v:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}\) are continuously differentiable functions, then
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b u(x)\,v'(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \left. u(x)\,v(x)\right|_a^b - \int_a^b u'(x)\,v(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}. \end{equation*}
    Integration by parts is typically used for integrals
    • of the form \(\int_a^b p(x)\, e^x\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) where \(p\) is a polynomial,
    • of the form \(\int_a^b p(x)\, \ln(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) where \(p\) is a polynomial,
    • with trigonometric functions, e.g., \(\int_a^b e^{c\,x}\sin(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{.}\)

    Example A.1.2. Integration by parts: Polynomial and exponential function.

    To evaluate \(\int_a^b x^2\,e^x\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\) we use integration by parts twice (each time with \(v'(x)=e^x=v(x)\) in the above formula) to obtain
    \begin{align*} \int_a^b x^2\,e^x\operatorname{d}\!{x} & = \left.x^2\,e^x\right|_a^b-\int_a^b 2x\,e^x\operatorname{d}\!{x} \\ & = \left.x^2\,e^x\right|_a^b -\left( \left.2x\,e^x\right|_a^b-\int_a^b2\,e^x\operatorname{d}\!{x}\right)\\ & = \left.\left(x^2-2x+2\right)\,e^x\right|_a^b \\ & = \left(b^2-2b+2\right)\,e^b-\left(a^2-2a+2\right)\,e^a. \end{align*}

    Example A.1.3. Integration by parts: Trigonometric functions.

    To evaluate \(\int_a^b \cos^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) we use \(u(x)=\cos(x)=v'(x)\) and \(\sin^2(x)=1-\cos^2(x)\) to obtain
    \begin{align*} \int_a^b \cos^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} & = \left.\cos(x)\,\sin(x)\right|_a^b-\int_a^b (-\sin(x))\,\sin(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} \\ & = \left.\cos(x)\,\sin(x)\right|_a^b+\int_a^b\sin^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} \\ & = \left.\cos(x)\,\sin(x)\right|_a^b+\int_a^b1-\cos^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} \\ & = \left.\cos(x)\,\sin(x)\right|_a^b+\int_a^b1\operatorname{d}\!{x}-\int_a^b\cos^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}, \end{align*}
    and thus
    \begin{equation*} 2\int_a^b \cos^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \left.\left(\cos(x)\,\sin(x)+x\right)\right|_a^b, \end{equation*}
    i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b \cos^2(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \frac12(b-a)+\frac12\cos(b)\,\sin(b)-\frac12\cos(a)\,\sin(a). \end{equation*}
    The chain rule for derivatives yields the following:

    Theorem A.1.4. Integration by Substitution.

    If \(u:[c,d]\to[a,b]\) is a continuously differentiable function and \(f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}\) is a continuous function, then
    \begin{equation*} \int_{u(c)}^{u(d)} f(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}= \int_c^d f(u(t))\,u'(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t}. \end{equation*}
    Note that there are two possibilities how to read this last formula: Either from left to right, or from right to left. We look at both these possibilities here.
    Reading the above formula from right to left, our task is the following: In order to evaluate \(\int_a^b g(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\) we try to write the integrand in the from \(g(x)=f(u(x))\,u'(x)\) with an appropriate function \(u\text{.}\) Often it is straight forward, at least with some practice and experience, to recognize this function \(u\text{.}\)

    Example A.1.5. Integration by substitution: Integrand is a product.

    \(\int_a^b x\,e^{-x^2/2}\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \int_{-\frac12a^2}^{-\frac12b^2} -e^u\operatorname{d}\!{u} = \left. -e^u\right|_{-\frac12a^2}^{-\frac12b^2} = -e^{-b^2/2}+e^{-a^2/2}\) where we use \(u(x)=-\frac{x^2}2\) and thus \(u'(x)=-x\text{,}\) \(u(a)=-\frac12a^2\) and \(u(b)=-\frac12b^2\text{.}\)

    Example A.1.6. Integration by substitution: Integrand is a function of sine and cosine.

    \begin{gather*} \int_a^b\tan(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \int_a^b\frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \int_{\cos(a)}^{\cos(b)} -\frac1{u}\operatorname{d}\!{u} \\ = \left.-\ln|{u}|\right|_{\cos(a)}^{\cos(b)}= -\ln|{\cos(b)}|+\ln|{\cos(a)}| = \ln\left|{\frac{\cos(a)}{\cos(b)}}\right| \end{gather*}
    where we use \(u(x)=\cos(x)\) and thus \(u'(x)=-\sin(x)\) (i.e., \(\tan(x)=-\frac{\cos'(x)}{\cos(x)}\)), \(u(a)=\cos(a)\) and \(u(b)=\cos(b)\text{.}\)
    We note that we also assumed that the cosine does not become zero in the interval \([a,b]\text{;}\) in particular, \(\cos(a)\) and \(\cos(b)\) must both be either positive or negative for the above formula to hold.
    Integrals of the form \(\int_a^b \sin(x)\,f(\cos(x))\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) and \(\int_a^b \cos(x)\,g(\sin(x))\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) (for some functions \(f,g\)) are evaluated similarly.
    An important special case here is:
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b\frac{u'(x)}{u(x)} \operatorname{d}\!{x} = \ldots = \ln\left|{\frac{u(b)}{u(a)}}\right| \end{equation*}
    as long as the continuously differentiable function \(u\) does not become zero in the interval \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    Reading the integration by substitution formula from left to right, our task is the following: In order to evaluate \(\int_a^b f(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\) we substitute \(u(t)\) for \(x\) where \(u:[c,d]\to [a,b]\) is a bijective (and therefore invertible), continuously differentiable function, to obtain
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^bf(x)\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \int_{u^{-1}(a)}^{u^{-1}(b)} f(u(t))\,u'(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t}. \end{equation*}
    Note that it might happen that for the inverse function \(u^{-1}\) we have \(u^{-1}(a)>u^{-1}(b)\text{.}\) Finding an appropriate substitution \(u\) here is often an art and requires lots of experience. In fact, one might consider it as one of the (most obvious) manifestitions showing that integration is much harder than differentiation.

    Example A.1.7. Integration by substitution: Integrand with square roots.

    In order to evaluate \(\int_a^b \frac1{\sqrt{x}}\ln(\sqrt{x})\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) with \(0 \lt a \lt b\text{,}\) we use \(x=u(t)=t^2\) (note that this function is bijective on the domain \([0,+\infty)\)). Then, \(\frac{\operatorname{d}\!{x}}{\operatorname{d}\!{t}}=u'(t)=2t\) and \(u^{-1}(t)=\sqrt{t}\) and thus
    \begin{align*} \int_a^b \frac1{\sqrt{x}}\ln(\sqrt{x})\operatorname{d}\!{x} & = \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} \frac1{t} \ln(t)\,2\,t\operatorname{d}\!{t} \\ & = \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} 2 \ln(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t} \ = \ 2 \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} \ln(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t} \\ & = \left.2\left(x\ln(x)-x\right)\right|_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} \end{align*}
    where we obtain \(\int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} \ln(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t}\) using integration by parts:
    \begin{align*} \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} \ln(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t} & = \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} 1\cdot \ln(t)\operatorname{d}\!{t} \\ & = \left.x\,\ln(x)\right|_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} - \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}}\frac{t}{t} \operatorname{d}\!{t}\ = \ \left.x\,\ln(x)\right|_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}} - \int_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}}1\operatorname{d}\!{t} \\ & = \left.\left(x\,\ln(x)-x\right)\right|_{\sqrt{a}}^{\sqrt{b}}. \end{align*}
    Obviously, the integral \(\int_a^b \frac1{\sqrt{x}}\ln(\sqrt{x})\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) can also (and usually, will) be solved by applying integration by substitution from right to left, in which case we take \(u(t)=\sqrt{t}\) and \(u'(t)=\frac1{2\sqrt{t}}\text{.}\) However, our goal here was to go the other way.

    Example A.1.8. Integration by substitution: Art at play.

    In order to evaluate \(\int_a^b \frac1{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) with \(0 \lt a \lt b\text{,}\) we use \(x=u(t) = \frac{t^2-1}{2t}\text{;}\) this function \(u: (0,+\infty)\to\mathbb{R}\) is strictly increasing and surjective, and therefore invertible. Its inverse function is \(u^{-1}(x)=x+\sqrt{1+x^2}\) (which is positive for all real \(x\)). Noting that \(\sqrt{1+x^2}=\frac{t^2+1}{2t}\text{,}\) we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\operatorname{d}\!{x}}{\operatorname{d}\!{t}}=\frac{t^2+1}{2\,t^2}=\frac{\sqrt{1+x^2}}{t}, \end{equation*}
    i.e., \(\frac{\operatorname{d}\!{t}}{t}=\frac{\operatorname{d}\!{x}}{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\text{.}\) Thus, we get
    \begin{align*} \int_a^b \frac1{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\operatorname{d}\!{x} & = \int_{a+\sqrt{1+a^2}}^{b+\sqrt{1+b^2}} \frac{1}{t}\operatorname{d}\!{t} \\ & = \left.\ln|{t}|\right|_{a+\sqrt{1+a^2}}^{b+\sqrt{1+b^2}} = \ln\left(\frac{b+\sqrt{1+b^2}}{a+\sqrt{1+a^2}}\right). \end{align*}
    As a side note, the derivative of the inverse hyperbolic sine \(\operatorname{arsinh}(x)\) is \(\operatorname{arsinh}'(x)=\frac1{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\text{;}\) thus, we have actually obtained an explicit formula for the inverse hyperbolic sine here, namely, \(\operatorname{arsinh}(x)=\ln(x+\sqrt{1+x^2})\text{,}\) and we can also write \(\int_a^b \frac1{\sqrt{1+x^2}}\operatorname{d}\!{x} = \operatorname{arsinh}(b) - \operatorname{arsinh}(a)\text{.}\)
    (Oh, and it is \(\operatorname{arsinh}(x)\text{,}\) not \(\operatorname{arcsinh}(x)\text{!}\) For hyperbolic trigonometric functions, it is an area not an arc length we are talking about!)
