    Section A.4 Special Integration Techniques: Trigonometric Substitutions (or the demise thereof)

    There are four types of integrals that we can effectively compute using trigonometric substitutions. They are
    • \(\int x^n R^k\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\)
    • \(\int \frac{x^n}{R^k}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\)
    • \(\int \frac{R^k}{x^n}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\) and
    • \(\int \frac1{x^n R^k}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{,}\)
    where \(n\ge 0\text{,}\) \(k\gt 0\) are integers, \(k\) is odd, and \(R\) has one of the following three forms
    • \(R = \sqrt{a^2-x^2}\text{;}\) in this case the domain of integration is contained in \([-a,a]\text{,}\)
    • \(R = \sqrt{a^2+x^2}\text{;}\) in this case the domain of integration is contained in \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\)
    • \(R = \sqrt{x^2-a^2}\text{;}\) in this case the domain of integration is contained either in \((-\infty,-a]\) or \([a,+\infty)\text{,}\)
    where \(a\in\mathbb{R}\) is some constant.
    Note that if \(k\) is even, we are in the situation of the last section, i.e., we have a rational function.

    Note A.4.2. Integrating functions with sine and cosine.

    In order to evaluate an integral of the form \(\int f(\cos(t),\sin(t))\operatorname{d}\!{t}\text{,}\) where \(f\) is some function, one can use yet another substitution: If we put \(u=\tan\left(\frac{t}2\right)\text{,}\) elementary trigonometry yields the simple formulas
    \begin{equation*} \sin(t) = \frac{2u}{1+u^2}\qquad \cos(t) = \frac{1-u^2}{1+u^2}, \end{equation*}
    and by differentiation we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\operatorname{d}\!{u}}{\operatorname{d}\!{t}}=\frac1{2\cos^2(t/2)}=\frac{1+u^2}2. \end{equation*}
    What is the result on the integrals we consider in this section? After the two substitutions we now arrived at an integral of the form \(\int \frac{p(u)}{q(u)}\operatorname{d}\!{u}\) where \(p\) and \(q\) are polynomials in \(u\text{,}\) i.e., the integrand is now a rational function in \(u\text{.}\) Using partial fractions, see the previous section (Section Section A.3), we can now evaluate this integral.
    This two-stage process should leave you wondering if we really need the trigonometric substitutions. After all, their only purpose, in some sense, is only to get rid of the quare root term before another substitution brings us back to Section A.3.
    In fact, the integrals of the four forms \(\int x^n R^k\operatorname{d}\!{x}, \ldots, \int \frac1{x^n R^k}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) can be computed using one of two

    Notice that the form of \(R\) does not play a role here, only the power \(n\) of the polynomial term.

    Example A.4.3. Integrand with odd \(n\).

    Let us evaluate \(\int\frac{x^3}{(9-x^2)^{5/2}}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{.}\) This is an integral of the form \(\int \frac{x^n}{R^k}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) where \(R=\sqrt{9-x^2}=\sqrt{3^2-x^2}\text{,}\) \(k=5\) and \(n=3\text{.}\) Since \(n\) is odd, we choose the substitution \(u^2=9-x^2\) which by implicit differentiation yields \(2u \frac{\operatorname{d}\!{u}}{\operatorname{d}\!{x}}=-2x\) and thus \(\operatorname{d}\!{x} = -\frac{u}{x}\operatorname{d}\!{u}\text{.}\) We therefore obtain
    \begin{align*} \int\frac{x^3}{(9-x^2)^{5/2}}\operatorname{d}\!{x} & = \int\frac{x^3}{u^{5}}\,\left(-\frac{u}{x}\right)\operatorname{d}\!{u}\\ & = -\int\frac{x^2}{u^{4}}\operatorname{d}\!{u}\\ & = -\int\frac{9-u^2}{u^{4}}\operatorname{d}\!{u}\\ & = -\int\left(\frac9{u^4}-\frac1{u^2}\right)\operatorname{d}\!{u}\\ & = -\left(-\frac13\cdot\frac9{u^3}+\frac1{u}\right)+C\\ & = \frac3{u^3}-\frac1{u}+C\\ & = \frac3{(\sqrt{9-x^2})^3}-\frac1{\sqrt{9-x^2}}+C. \end{align*}

    Example A.4.4. Integrand with even \(n\).

    Let us evaluate \(\int\frac{\sqrt{16+x^2}}{x^4}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\text{.}\) This is an integral of the form \(\int \frac{R^k}{x^n}\operatorname{d}\!{x}\) where \(R=\sqrt{16+x^2}=\sqrt{4^2+x^2}\text{,}\) \(k=1\) and \(n=4\text{.}\) Since \(n\) is even, we choose the substitution \(u^2=\frac{16+x^2}{x^2}=\frac{16}{x^2}+1\) which by implicit differentiation yields \(2u \frac{\operatorname{d}\!{u}}{\operatorname{d}\!{x}}=-\frac{32}{x^3}\) and thus \(\operatorname{d}\!{x} = -\frac{x^3u}{16}\operatorname{d}\!{u}\text{.}\) Noting that \(x^2 u^2=R^2=16+x^2\text{,}\) and thus \(\sqrt{16+x^2}=x\,u\text{,}\) we obtain
    \begin{align*} \int\frac{\sqrt{16+x^2}}{x^4}\operatorname{d}\!{x} & = \int\frac{x\,u}{x^{4}}\,\left(-\frac{x^3 u}{16}\right)\operatorname{d}\!{u}\\ & = -\frac1{16}\int {u^{2}} \operatorname{d}\!{u}\\ & = -\frac1{48} u^3+C\\ & = -\frac1{48} \frac{(\sqrt{16+x^2})^3}{x^3}+C. \end{align*}
    Notice that the resulting integrands in \(u\) are, as expected, again rational functions. As a consequence, compare the previous Section A.3, the resulting integrals in \(u\) involve only linear combinations of integral power functions, the functions \(\ln|1+u|\text{,}\) \(\ln|1-u|\text{,}\) and the inverse tangent function \(\arctan(u)\text{.}\) In particular, we do not need the inverse sine or the inverse secant functions
